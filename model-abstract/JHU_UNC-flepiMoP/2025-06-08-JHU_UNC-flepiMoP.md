## Summary of results
For Round 20 we submit the JHU_UNC flepiMoP2 model, a Bayesian continuous-age
compartmental model rebuilt on the flepiMoP2 pipeline and the op_system model
specification. Across all five scenarios the model projects recurring COVID-19
activity with both a summer and a winter wave in each season of the projection
period. Differences among scenarios are driven by the breadth and frequency of
vaccination: broader eligibility (all eligible groups, scenarios B/D) and the
addition of a second, spring booster for high-risk groups (scenarios C/E) avert
more hospitalizations and deaths than the counterfactual with no further
vaccination after mid-February 2026 (scenario A), with the largest scenario
separation concentrated around the summer 2026 wave that the spring campaign is
designed to blunt. Because vaccination in this model acts primarily by reducing
susceptibility and severity through the immune-history ladder, the averted
burden scales with both coverage level (B vs D) and dosing frequency (B/D vs
C/E).

## Explanation of observed dynamics given model assumptions
The recurring summer/winter wave structure is generated by a
location-hierarchical seasonal transmission term combined with waning of
immunity and continuous immune escape: as protection wanes and circulating
strains drift away from prior exposures, the susceptible pool is replenished on
a timescale that, together with the fitted seasonal forcing, produces
semi-annual peaks in many jurisdictions. Scenario differences emerge because
vaccination moves individuals up the immune-history ladder (reducing infection
risk) and, when vaccinated, also reduces the probability of hospitalization and
death; scenarios with more people vaccinated or with an additional spring dose
for high-risk individuals therefore keep more of the population in
higher-protection states through the summer 2026 wave. The magnitude of
scenario separation is tempered by immune escape and waning, which erode
vaccine-derived protection over the projection horizon.

## Model assumptions: please describe:
### Number/type of immune classes considered
Population is stratified by continuous age (a 15-node age grid spanning 0-100
years), low/high risk, vaccination status (unvaccinated/vaccinated), and an
11-rung immune-history ladder (levels x0-x10). Level x10 denotes maximal
protection (e.g., immediately following infection or vaccination) and level x0
denotes full susceptibility. Individuals move up the ladder upon infection and
vaccination and descend it through waning and antigenic drift. This is combined
with the standard SEIR flow (susceptible/exposed/infectious with Erlang-staged
compartments) plus hospitalization and death pathways.

### Initial distribution of susceptibility (if available)
Initial conditions are seeded from a preprocessed per-jurisdiction immune-ladder
prior and redistributed across the ladder rungs, then the model is run through a
multi-year burn-in (simulation start 2022-07-03, with the likelihood beginning
after an initial burn-in) so that the initial-condition assumptions are largely
washed out before the calibration and projection windows.

### Initial variant characteristics (transmissibility of variants at t=0, and how uncertainty or non-identifiability was handled)
Distinct variants are not tracked explicitly; instead antigenic change is
represented by a continuous immune-escape process. Baseline transmissibility is
parameterized through a per-contact transmission scale and a
location-hierarchical seasonal R0(t); its uncertainty and partial
non-identifiability against seasonality and reporting are handled by full
Bayesian inference (a structured variational posterior) rather than point
estimates.

### Details about calibration of immunity at t=0 (calibration period considered, assumptions about/fitting of past immune escape and waning immunity, is the same calibration process used for all scenarios?)
Immunity at t=0 is set from the preprocessed immune-ladder prior and then
refined implicitly by fitting the transmission, waning, and escape parameters to
the observed 2022-2026 hospitalization and death record. Waning and immune
escape are inferred (not fixed) during calibration. The same single calibration
is used for every scenario; scenarios differ only in their future vaccination
coverage.

### Details about modeling of immune escape after t=0 (including what is the level of immune escape considered, whether a stepwise or continuous escape was considered and how immune escape affects infection- and vaccine-induced immunity)
Immune escape is continuous (a smooth, coarse-knot multi-year envelope applied
to the force of infection), not stepwise. Because this model does not
distinguish infection- from vaccine-derived immunity (both are represented by
position on the shared immune-history ladder), escape erodes protection from
both sources symmetrically by increasing the effective susceptibility of
higher-ladder rungs over time.

### Assumptions regarding waning immunity against infection and symptoms (including values used for the duration and level of protection against infection or symptomatic disease, whether a point estimate was used or a range, and distribution used)
Waning is represented as movement down the immune-history ladder at an inferred
rate (informed by a prior in the 3-10 month range suggested for the round);
rather than fixing a single duration, the waning rate is given a prior
distribution and its posterior is estimated jointly with transmission. Waned
individuals retain partial protection until they reach the lowest rungs, i.e.
protection declines toward, but is bounded by, a fitted susceptibility plateau.

### Assumptions regarding waning immunity against severe disease (including whether immunity against severe disease, conditional on infection, is fixed vs declines over time; and if it wanes, specify how)
Protection against severe disease is modeled primarily through protection
against infection (ladder position) plus a vaccination-status severity
reduction. The vaccination-conditional severity reduction is treated as fixed
(it does not wane on its own), so declining protection against severe outcomes
over time arises from waning/escape acting on the infection pathway and from
individuals leaving the vaccinated state.

### Assumptions regarding boosting effect from multiple infections
Repeated infections boost immunity only through the ladder mechanism: each
infection returns an individual to the top protection rung (x10). There is no
additional, separate multiplicative boosting effect beyond this reset.

### Is vaccination assumed to prevent infection and/or transmissibility?
Vaccination prevents infection (susceptibility reduction, implemented by moving
vaccinees up the immune-history ladder) and additionally reduces the
probability of hospitalization and death given infection. Reduced transmission
arises indirectly through the reduction in infections; there is no separate
explicit reduction of per-contact infectiousness for breakthrough infections.

### How is re-vaccination of individuals handled in scenarios where high-risk individuals can be vaccinated twice a year (eg, is a spring dose only given to individuals who had a prior fall dose)?
Vaccination is applied as a rate on the unvaccinated-to-vaccinated transition
driven by the scenario-specific coverage curves indexed by time, age, and risk.
In the spring-campaign scenarios (C/E) the additional high-risk dose is applied
through the provided high-risk spring coverage; we do not explicitly condition
the spring dose on having received the prior fall dose (doses are modeled at the
population/coverage level rather than by tracking individual dose histories), so
the spring campaign raises protection among high-risk individuals up to the
provided coverage.

### Are vaccination curves used as provided, or was there any adjustment made?
The SMH-provided vaccination-coverage curves are used as provided for each
scenario, interpolated onto the model time grid and mapped to the
unvaccinated-to-vaccinated transition rates by age and risk.

### Was there any adjustment made to scenario specifications beyond vaccine coverage? 
No. Beyond the scenario-specific vaccination coverage (and, for C/E, the
additional spring high-risk campaign), no other scenario parameters were
altered.

### How are projections generated for the retrospective period (June 2025-May 2026)?
The model is a single continuous simulation from the calibration period through
the end of the projection horizon; the retrospective year is produced by the
same mechanistic forward run under each scenario's vaccination assumptions.
Because all available hospitalization and death data through the present were
used for calibration, the retrospective year is a counterfactual consistent with
recent observations rather than an out-of-sample forecast.

### Describe the process used to set or calibrate disease severity, ie P(hosp given current infection) and P(death given current infection) details. What are the datasets used for calibration of the death targets?
Severity is modeled with smooth, age-resolved infection-hospitalization and
hospitalization-fatality functions (a parametric age curve) combined with
hierarchical per-jurisdiction severity multipliers and slow multiplicative time
trends, plus a vaccination-status severity reduction. These severity parameters
are inferred jointly with transmission by fitting weekly hospital admissions
(NHSN and NSSP age-specific admissions) and weekly deaths (NCHS, as distributed
in the SMH target data). Death targets are calibrated against the state and
national weekly death series.

### Describe seasonality implementation, e.g., whether seasonality varies by geography, what is the function used to model seasonal forcing, and which datasets are used to fit seasonal parameters
Seasonal forcing is a low-order Fourier function of time (annual, semi-annual,
and ter-annual harmonics) applied to R0(t). It varies by geography through a
non-centered partial-pooling hierarchy: each jurisdiction shares a common
seasonal shape but carries its own phase and amplitude, so summer-dominant and
winter-dominant states can be represented simultaneously. Seasonal parameters
are fit to the observed 2022-2026 hospitalization and death series (no external
climate covariate is used); a slow secular trend and time-varying coefficients
are separated from the annual cycle.

### What is the calibration period used to fit the model? Are there any adjustments made to the reported NHSN hospitalization data?
The model is fit to data from mid-2022 through the most recent available week
(with a burn-in before the likelihood window). NHSN hospitalization data are
used as provided; the May-October 2024 voluntary-reporting dip is handled by
down-weighting recent-vs-older weeks through the likelihood recency weighting
rather than by editing the reported values, and no reporting-coverage inflation
factor was applied for this submission.

### Details about modeling of age-specific outcomes, including assumptions on age-specific parameters (e.g., susceptibility, infection hospitalization risk or fatality risk, VE)
Age is continuous. Infection-hospitalization and infection-fatality risks are
smooth functions of age (rising with age, with an elevated infant component),
shared across risk groups. Age-specific contact structure enters through the
contact kernel (below). Susceptibility to infection is governed by position on
the immune-history ladder rather than an intrinsic age term. Outputs are
aggregated from the continuum to the requested SMH age groups (0-130 plus 0-4,
5-17, 18-64 low-risk, 18-64 high-risk, and 65+); the finer bands sum exactly to
the all-age total.

### Details about modeling of high-risk individuals, e.g., susceptibility and infection hospitalization risk or infection fatality risk, VE
The population carries a low/high-risk split (with high-risk defined per the
SMH-provided high-/low-risk population and coverage data). In this model the
risk split is used chiefly to target vaccination: high-risk individuals follow
their own coverage curves and are the group eligible for the additional spring
booster in scenarios C/E. Per-infection severity is modeled as a function of age
shared across risk strata, so differences in outcomes between risk groups arise
from their differing vaccination coverage and resulting immune protection rather
than from a separate intrinsic high-risk severity multiplier.

### Is empirical data on human mobility or contact patterns used in the model?
Empirical contact-pattern data are used: age mixing is represented by a
parametric age-age contact kernel (preschool, school, adult, and parent-child
components) calibrated to a USA-specific Prem synthetic per-capita contact
matrix and stored as a rank-6 low-rank factorization. Human mobility data are
not used.

### Is there a background level of non pharmaceutical interventions?
There is no explicit, separately parameterized NPI term. The net effect of
behavior and any interventions is absorbed into the inferred, time-varying,
location-hierarchical transmission term R0(t) and its secular trend.

### Is importation from other countries considered?
No. Importation from outside the United States is not explicitly modeled;
transmission is driven by within- and between-age-group mixing within each
jurisdiction.

### Other updates in model assumptions from previous rounds (e.g., demographic dynamics, immune escape, severity)
This round uses a substantially rebuilt model implemented on the flepiMoP2
pipeline with the op_system model specification. Key changes from prior rounds
include: a continuous-age formulation with a parametric age-age contact kernel
(rank-6 low-rank factorization) replacing coarse discrete age bins; a
location-hierarchical low-order Fourier seasonality with non-centered partial
pooling and a separated secular trend (replacing monthly seasonal terms); a
continuous immune-escape envelope; and fully Bayesian inference by stochastic
variational inference with a structured low-rank multivariate-normal guide
(warm-started from MAP and a Laplace approximation), with LogNormal-on-rates
observation models and first-order autoregressive residual correlation
(phi ~ 0.92 for hospitalizations, ~ 0.80 for deaths). Severity is age-resolved
with hierarchical per-jurisdiction multipliers and slow time trends.
